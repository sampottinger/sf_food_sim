SF Food Availability Simulation

Cities impact our health in lots of ways, including how they provide access to nutrition. Consider food deserts which are low income areas 1 mile or more away from healthy affordable food. These regions are important for public health because, for residents in these areas, it can be challenging to maintain a good diet.

What does food access look like in one of the wealthiest cities in the US? This map shows all of the areas with housing within San Francisco. This guide will walk through different neighborhoods in the city and also give you a chance to explore what behavior changes or new construction can help with food access.

These filled in dots () represent supermarkets. The denser northern part of the city offers many food options but there are some locations in SF with less access, especially in the less dense southern part of the city. For example, the simulation has circled Hunter's Point in the Bayview-Hunter's Point neighborhood which, in addition to being one of the poorer areas, is far from a supermarket.
<< Previous / Next >>
Of course, supermarkets aren't the only source of food and these filled in squares () represent fast food locations. This tool simulates if different residential areas would choose to go to fast food or the supermarket based on distance to each. Note how fast food dominates the south east of SF and, for a lot of residents in this poorer area, fast food simply represents a much more convenient option.
<< Previous / Next >>
Of course, one might assume that this just reflects being further from the city center but income may matter here too. In contrast to the Bayview, the Twin Peaks area just to the west is both wealthier and offers more supermarket options. This demonstrates an interesting trend where sometimes income follows access: those potentially least equipped to travel to supermarkets are also sometimes those furthest from them.
<< Previous / Next >>
Finally, food deserts are often framed in terms of physical distance and income but public transit also plays a role. Consider the Sunset. While higher income relative to the Bayview, it sees poor transportation access, indicating another way the layout of the city might "push" parts of its population to less healthy food options.

Now that you know what some of the problems are, how might you make it better? To simulate public policy encouraging development of new food sources (like subsidies), use these controls to add new supermarkets (or fast food locations). Also, use the slider to simulate programs (like transit subsidies) which might encourage residents to travel further to get to a supermarket relative to the nearest fast food option. You may also consider reviewing maps of income in the area, how neighborhood wealth has changed over time, and the layout of SF's mass transit.

Uses fast food and supermarkets from OpenStreetMap under the ODBL license, filtering only for those with non-empty names to remove potential erroneous data. If the distance between a supermarket / fast food option is less than 0.1 miles, the supermarket is "chosen" by the "house" (ring) regardless of distance tollerance. Distances are straight line (as the crow flies) distance.

Open source code and data available (MIT, ODBL). Simulation uses proj4js under the MIT license as well as PapaParse under the MIT license. Some StackOverflow used under the CC-BY-SA license (superliminary, Nathan Lippi). Uses ColorBrewer.

Preprocessing in Python uses Jupyter under the BSD license and Shapely under the BSD license.
